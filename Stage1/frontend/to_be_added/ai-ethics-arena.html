The framework

Every dilemma runs on the
same four-step structure.

A consistent rhythm that students internalize after two cases — then apply to dilemmas they encounter in the wild.

STEP 01

Read the case

A 200-word scenario drawn from real headlines: biased hiring tools, facial recognition in schools, deepfakes, content moderation.

STEP 02

Take a role

Argue from one of three stakeholder positions: the engineer, the affected user, or the regulator. Rotate each round.

STEP 03

Build the case

Use the structured worksheet: claim, evidence, harms, benefits, trade-offs. Cite principles from the UNESCO AI Ethics framework.

STEP 04

Defend & reflect

Classroom debate. Then a private reflection prompt: would you change your mind if you were the affected party instead?

12 dilemmas

Real cases. Real stakes.
No easy answers.

Each dilemma is mapped to one of five ethics domains and comes with discussion prompts, stakeholder briefs, and a facilitator guide.

Bias ⚖️

The Resume Filter

A Fortune 500 deploys an AI hiring tool that quietly downgrades women's resumes. Who's responsible — the engineers, the company, or the algorithm?

Bias 🏥

The Health Score

A healthcare AI trained on urban populations misdiagnoses rural patients. Should it be deployed while the dataset is being fixed?

Fairness 🎓

The College Admissions Bot

An AI scores applicants on "potential." It favors one high school over another. Is the AI being fair if the high school correlation is real?

Fairness 🚖

The Surge Price

A ride-hailing AI charges low-income neighborhoods 30% more because demand patterns allow it. Legal? Fair? Both? Neither?

Privacy 📷

The School Camera

A school installs facial recognition "for safety." It works. It also logs every student's location every 30 seconds. Trade the privacy for the safety?

Privacy 💬

The Chat Monitor

A chat app scans all DMs for self-harm keywords — and saves the matches for advertising. Where's the line between care and surveillance?

Accountability 🚗

The Self-Driving Crash

An autonomous car swerves to avoid a child and hits a cyclist. Who is charged — the owner, the manufacturer, or the AI?

Accountability 📝

The Ghostwritten Essay

A student submits an AI-written essay and gets an A. The teacher can't tell. Is it plagiarism if the AI isn't a person?

Citizenship 🎭

The Deepfake Principal

A student posts a convincing deepfake of the principal announcing a snow day. Funny? Or fraud? Where's the line?

Citizenship 🗳️

The Bot Swarm

A political campaign deploys 10,000 AI accounts to amplify talking points. Should the platform ban them? Should the law?

Why ethics, why now

The next generation will deploy AI.
They need to argue about it first.

Today's middle-schoolers will graduate into a workforce where AI makes hiring, lending, healthcare, and policing decisions. The Ethics Arena gives them practice reasoning about those decisions before they're the ones making them.

  • Every case is drawn from a real headline — no thought experiments.
  • Stakeholder rotation forces students to argue positions they don't hold.
  • Aligned to UNESCO AI Ethics recommendations + CSTA K-12 standards.
